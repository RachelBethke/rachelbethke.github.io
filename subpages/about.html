background

My first exposure to both aerospace and computer science came during my junior year of high school. I took a computer science principles class and joined my school’s Astronaut Challenge team at a friend’s request. Both experiences ended up shaping what I wanted to pursue.
The following year, I became captain of the team. We were the first all-female team to compete and earned my school’s first overall win. At the same time, I was taking AP Computer Science, which confirmed that I wanted to continue studying CS in college.

cuair

As an autopilot software developer on Cornell Unmanned Air Systems, I’ve worked on projects of varying scope, all centered around student-built autonomous VTOL UAVs, and it’s been one of the most rewarding parts of college.
Beyond writing code, I’ve learned how to work on cross-functional teams with mechanical and electrical engineers, tackle underdefined problems, and test software on real hardware. I've even been used as extra hands for the mechanical team during drone fabrication! More than anything, I’ve learned how to enjoy the process and the importance of working on projects I'm passionate about.

track & field

I compete in the 400m dash as a sprinter for Cornell's NCAA D1 team. Although injuries have kept me from running off and on for most of my collegiate career, I love the sport. I think juggling practices, classes, CUAir, and TA responsibilities has given me a discipline I could only have gotten through division I athletics. I know how to work well on teams with people from different backgrounds, respond well to feedback, and know how to thrive under pressure.
